Heads and Hills Simulator

Calculating how fast your grandmother will tumble down the stairs is now a problem of past with the all new Heads and Hills Simulator! Just use the tool below to precisely calculate and graph all sorts of physics problems related to inclined planes and force.

This simulation was designed to accurately demonstrate the effect of a variety of different factors on an object. These factors include: added force, mass, slope, gravity, and friction. Just how important is each of these variables in affecting the velocity of a sliding object?

The velocity at which a decapitated head below will slide across a plane is dependent on the following:

  • The force pushing the head across the plane.
  • The mass of the head.
  • The slope of the plane.
  • The acceleration of gravity.
  • The friction of the plane.
Given these factors, we can use some fundemental laws of physics to derive expressions that will tell us the distance traveled and the velocity of the head at any moment in time. The two equations used in the following calculations are:

force = mass × acceleration (Newtons Second Law of Motion)
distance = 0.5 × acceleration × time2 (constant acceleration)
distance = velocity × time

The presentation of this research project is done entirely with html/css and vanilla javascript. No third party libraries or packages are used. The source code for the simulation computations and design can be found by clicking the orange "Code" button below.

The process of generating the simulation can be divided into three steps:

  1. Calculating all the forces acting on the object.

    parallel_force: Fp = added_force + mass × g × sin(θ)
    frictional_force: Ff = m × g × cos(θ) × frictional_coefficient
    total_force = parallel_force - frictional_force

  2. Calculating the acceleration using it to compute distance.

    acceleration = total_force ÷ mass
    distance = acceleration × time2

  3. Using distance and time to calculate velocity.

    velocity = distance ÷ time

The simulation below uses the physics outlined above to calculate the distance the head will travel and its velocity every millisecond. The results are then shown through the animation and the graphs.
