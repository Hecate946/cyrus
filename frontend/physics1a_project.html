Investigating Motion Dynamics and Impact of Parameters

By Cyrus Asasi

The present research project aims to develop a comprehensive model to analyze the behavior of an object sliding up or down an inclined plane. The research focuses on understanding the physics involved in the sliding process and exploring the influence of various parameters on the object's object's trajectory, velocity and acceleration. The simulation will be based on fundamental principles of classical mechanics and will consider the impact of key factors such as incline angle, object mass, additional forces applied, and coefficient of friction between the object and the inclined surface. Additionally, the simulation will allow for the acceleration due to gravity to be manually set, permitting users to view the time-dependent behavior of the sliding object under the gravitational force of planetary bodies different from Earth.

The study of motion and its underlying forces has been a fundamental aspect of physics for centuries. Using my background in Computer Science, my goal was to craft an interactive simulation using javascript that can be used as a tool to visualize and understand the physics behind all motion.

Objectives:
  • Motion Dynamics: The primary goal of this research project is to facilitate a deeper understanding of motion dynamics. By interacting with the simulation, users will gain insights into how various forces interact to influence the motion of the sliding block. The simulation presents an intuitive tool to understand the implications of Newton's Second Law of Motion and the Kinematic Equations.
  • Frictional Effects: Friction plays a critical role in the motion of objects on inclined surfaces. Through the simulation, users will be able to observe how different frictional coefficients affect the object's speed, acceleration, and distance traveled.
  • Motion Graphs: To further illustrate the behavior of the object, the simulation will automatically generate two fundamental motion graphs: distance vs. time and velocity vs. time. Users can directly observe and analyze the relationship between these graphs and the inputted parameters, gaining valuable insights into how the physics behind the simulation influences inclined-plane problems in the real world.
The presentation of this research project is done entirely with html/css and vanilla javascript. No third party libraries or packages are used. The source code for the simulation computations and design can be found by clicking the orange "Code" button below or by visiting the following github link: https://github.com/hecate946/hecate946.com

The velocity at which an object will slide across a plane is dependent on the following factors:

  • The force pushing the object across the plane.
  • The mass of the object.
  • The slope of the plane.
  • The acceleration of gravity.
  • The friction of the plane.
Given these factors, we can use some fundamental laws of physics to derive expressions that will tell us the distance traveled and the velocity of the object at any moment in time. The equations used in the following calculations are:

force = mass × acceleration (Newton's Second Law of Motion)
distance = 0.5 × acceleration × time2
distance = velocity × time

The process of generating the simulation can be divided into three steps:

  1. Calculating all the forces acting on the object.

    parallel_force: Fp = added_force + mass × g × sin(θ)
    frictional_force: Ff = m × g × cos(θ) × frictional_coefficient
    total_force = parallel_force - frictional_force

  2. Calculating the acceleration using it to compute distance.

    acceleration = total_force ÷ mass
    distance = acceleration × time2

  3. Using distance and time to calculate velocity.

    velocity = distance ÷ time

The simulation uses the physics outlined above to calculate the distance the object will travel and its velocity at each millisecond. The results are then dynamically animated into the simulation and graphs using css and javascript

From the simulation of a block sliding down an incline, users can draw several important results and observations related to motion dynamics and frictional effects. Some of the key results that users can analyze and interpret include:
  1. Effect of Incline Angle: Users can observe how the incline angle influences the block's motion. Steeper inclines will result in faster acceleration and a shorter distance covered by the block compared to shallower inclines.
  2. Impact of Applied Force: By adjusting the magnitude of the applied force, users can explore how external forces affect the block's motion. They will observe that a larger applied force leads to greater acceleration and faster velocity, while a smaller force results in slower motion.
  3. Frictional Effects: The simulation allows users to investigate the role of friction in the object's motion. Users can vary the frictional coefficients of the surface to observe how friction affects the object's speed, acceleration, and overall distance traveled. They will discover that higher frictional coefficients result in reduced motion and increased time taken to reach the simulation's 10 meter distance.
  4. Velocity and Distance Graphs: The automatically generated velocity vs. time and distance vs. time graphs provide valuable insights into the object's motion over time. Users can analyze the slopes and shapes of these graphs to understand how various parameters impact the object's acceleration and displacement.
  5. Relationship between Parameters: Users can explore the relationships between different input parameters, such as incline angle, applied force, mass of the object, and frictional coefficients. By varying one parameter while keeping others constant, they can observe how changes in one variable affect the overall motion of the object.
  6. Motion under Different Conditions: Users can run simulations with different combinations of parameters to observe the object's behavior under varying conditions. For example, they can compare the motion of a object on a frictionless incline to one with significant friction to understand how real-world scenarios differ from idealized cases. They could also contrast how a certain object will behave when acted on by the gravitational pull of different planets. For instance, a user could use the simulation to see how an object sliding down a hill on Jupiter will be different from that same block sliding down a hill on Earth.
Overall, the simulation provides an interactive way for users to explore the interaction of forces and motion on an inclined plane. Through observation, users can see fundamental physics principles in action, gain practical insights into the significance of friction, and develop an appreciation for the factors involved in motion dynamics.
